### Seeing the Invisible: What is X-ray Diffraction?
All matter around us is made up of incredibly tiny "molecules" that are too small to see with the naked eye. Knowing the shape of these molecules and how they are arranged (their crystal structure) is crucial for understanding the properties of materials and for creating new, useful substances. However, since molecules are so small, we cannot see them with ordinary microscopes. This is where a technique called "X-ray diffraction" comes into play.

### Unlocking Molecular "Addresses" and "Shapes" with X-rays
X-ray diffraction is an experimental method that involves shining special X-rays onto a crystal and analyzing how they scatter (the diffraction pattern). This allows us to precisely determine the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms (3D coordinates), the size of the basic repeating unit of the crystal (cell parameters), and the symmetry with which molecules are arranged within the crystal (space group). It's like inferring the shape of an object from its shadow, allowing us to accurately pinpoint the "address" and "shape" of each molecule. In this study, the crystal structure of a molecule was determined in detail using this X-ray diffraction experiment.

### Crystal Structure Data Paving the Way for Future Chemistry
The obtained crystal structure data is not just about revealing beautiful molecular shapes; it provides essential fundamental information for predicting the properties of the substance (e.g., whether it conducts electricity or has medicinal effects) and for designing new materials with even better functions. The crystal structure data determined in this research has been deposited into the Cambridge Structural Database (CCDC), a global repository for small molecule crystal structures.

### Shared Knowledge Advances Science
Databases like CCDC are invaluable resources that researchers worldwide can freely access and utilize. By sharing the new molecular shapes and arrangements we discover, other researchers can build upon this knowledge to make new discoveries or develop more efficient materials. Thus, openly publishing and sharing data obtained from precise experiments plays a crucial role in accelerating the progress of chemistry and science as a whole.
