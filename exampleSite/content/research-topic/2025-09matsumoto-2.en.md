### The Key to Climate Change Solutions: Challenges in CO2 Transport

To combat global warming, technologies like Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) and Carbon Capture and Utilization (CCU) are gaining attention. These involve capturing CO2 from industrial sources and either storing it underground or converting it into useful substances. For these technologies to work, large quantities of CO2 must be transported safely. Often, CO2 is transported as a liquid through pipelines or by ships. However, this transport faces a significant challenge: even a tiny amount of "water" mixed with the CO2 can cause problems.

### The Troublesome "CO2 Hydrate"

When water and CO2 meet under specific conditions (low temperatures and high pressures), a substance called "CO2 hydrate" can form. This is a type of "ice" where water molecules create cage-like structures that trap CO2 molecules inside. If CO2 hydrate adheres to the walls of pipelines or completely blocks them, CO2 transport can grind to a halt. Furthermore, it can lead to corrosion of the transport vessels.

### Unraveling Invisible Water Behavior with "Chemical Potential"

To ensure the safe transport of CO2, it is crucial to accurately know how much water dissolves in liquid CO2 (solubility) and how that solubility changes when CO2 hydrate forms. However, studying water behavior in detail experimentally under the extreme conditions required for liquid CO2 (high pressure) or hydrate formation (low temperature) is very difficult.
Therefore, we used theoretical calculations to determine the "chemical potential," an indicator of a substance's stability and its tendency to move between different phases (liquid, solid, etc.). A key advantage of this method is its applicability to extreme states, such as deeply cooled and heavily compressed conditions where CO2 hydrate forms. We also confirmed that our calculations successfully reproduce experimental trends in water solubility.

### Hydrates Dramatically Reduce Water Solubility!

Our calculations revealed a striking fact: when CO2 hydrate coexists with water dissolved in liquid CO2, the amount of water that can dissolve in liquid CO2 significantly decreases compared to when no hydrate is present.
This happens because water is more stable (has a lower chemical potential) when trapped within the hydrate structure than when dissolved in liquid CO2. In essence, when hydrate forms, liquid CO2 loses its capacity to retain water, leading to even more water precipitating as hydrate at low temperatures.
This discovery provides valuable information for solving serious problems in massive CO2 transport, such as pipeline blockage and vessel corrosion. Our research demonstrates the excitement of "theoretical chemistry," using the power of computers to unravel the rules of the invisible molecular world and contribute to solving societal challenges.
