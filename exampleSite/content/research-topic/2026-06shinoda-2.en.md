### The Challenge of Delivering Drugs into Cells

Many medium-sized therapeutics, such as nucleic acids and peptides, face a significant hurdle in reaching their targets: the cell membrane. This membrane acts as a robust barrier, making efficient intracellular delivery a major challenge in medicine.

Our research group previously developed a light-driven molecular machine based on a tetraphenylethene derivative (TPE-C8-N) that can achieve carrier-free intracellular delivery of biomolecules under light irradiation. This system offers a promising solution to overcome the membrane barrier. However, the precise molecular mechanism by which this photoresponsive TPE derivative induces membrane permeabilization (the ability of the membrane to let substances pass through) remained unclear.

### Unlocking Cell Membranes with Light: The TPE Molecule's Secret

In this study, we combined cellular experiments with multiscale molecular dynamics (MD) simulations, path-sampling, and umbrella-sampling simulations. This comprehensive approach allowed us to establish a structure-dependent strategy for controlling membrane permeabilization induced by photoresponsive TPE derivatives.

A key focus was to examine the effect of the alkyl chain length on intracellular delivery. We synthesized a series of TPE derivatives, TPE-Cn-N (where 'n' represents the number of carbon atoms in the alkyl chain: n = 4, 8, 12, and 16), to systematically investigate this factor.

### Alkyl Chain Length Dictates Drug Delivery Pathways

Cellular experiments revealed that TPE-C8-N, with an alkyl chain length of 8, localized efficiently to the plasma membrane and exhibited the highest endocytosis-independent delivery efficiency. Endocytosis is a process where cells engulf substances by forming vesicles from the cell membrane. This suggests TPE-C8-N can directly modify the membrane. In contrast, longer-chain derivatives tended to form intracellular aggregates and relied more strongly on endocytosis-mediated uptake. This demonstrated that the alkyl chain length enables switching between distinct intracellular delivery pathways.

Coarse-grained MD simulations, which model molecules in a simplified way to observe larger-scale behaviors, showed that the alkyl chain length governs the self-assembly morphology of TPE molecules on the membrane. Specifically, TPE-C8-N formed linear assemblies that induced pronounced membrane curvature (bending).

Furthermore, rare-event simulations (which focus on infrequent but important molecular events) using ∞RETIS and umbrella sampling techniques showed that TPE-C8-N modestly promotes the nucleation (formation) of transient pores (temporary holes) and accumulates more strongly near these pores than TPE-C16-N. This suggests that multiple TPE-C8-N molecules can cooperatively enhance the membrane perturbation induced by photoisomerization (a change in molecular structure caused by light). Importantly, the simulations indicated that TPE-C8-N does not stabilize expanded open pores, supporting a mechanism of transient membrane-defect formation rather than persistent pore stabilization as the primary permeabilization mechanism.

### Designing Future Light-Driven Delivery Systems

These results provide crucial molecular-level and thermodynamic insights into light-induced membrane permeabilization. They establish clear design guidelines for developing more effective light-driven intracellular delivery systems. This research has significant implications for advancing targeted drug delivery in various therapeutic and diagnostic applications, opening new avenues for future medical treatments.
