### Cosmic Explosions: Supernova Remnants and Gamma Rays

In the vast universe, massive stars conclude their lives with spectacular explosions, leaving behind expanding structures known as "supernova remnants (SNRs)". Within these remnants, extremely high-energy particles interact, emitting powerful "gamma rays" – a form of electromagnetic radiation invisible to our eyes. Gamma rays serve as crucial clues, revealing the universe's most violent phenomena.

### Two Gamma-Ray Sources Captured by the Fermi Telescope

Our research focused on the gamma-ray emission from the vicinity of the composite supernova remnant "CTB 87". By analyzing over 16 years of data collected by NASA's "Fermi Large Area Telescope", we discovered two distinct gamma-ray sources in this region, each with different characteristics.

The first source exhibited a "soft" gamma-ray spectrum (energy distribution). We propose this emission likely arises from interactions between the supernova remnant and "molecular clouds (MCs)" – interstellar aggregations of gas and dust, rich in molecules like hydrogen.

The second source showed a "hard" gamma-ray spectrum, which aligns with the TeV gamma-ray source "VER J2016+371" observed by another telescope (VERITAS). This hard gamma-ray emission is highly likely produced by a "pulsar wind nebula (PWN)" within CTB 87, driven by the powerful particle wind emanating from a rapidly rotating neutron star (pulsar).

### The Power of "Spectroscopic Chemistry" in Unraveling Cosmic Mysteries

To explain the behavior of these gamma-ray sources, we proposed a theoretical framework called the "leptonic model". This model describes how elementary particles like electrons (leptons) gain energy and emit gamma rays. Using this model, we achieved results consistent with multi-wavelength observations, from radio waves to X-rays.

At first glance, this research might seem to belong solely to astrophysics. However, the analysis of the energy distribution (spectrum) of light (electromagnetic waves) to investigate the state and origin of matter is deeply connected to "spectroscopic chemistry". By analyzing high-energy gamma rays, we can understand the properties of molecular clouds in space and the behavior of matter (such as electrons) under extreme conditions.

Even the most magnificent cosmic phenomena reveal their nature by allowing us to decipher the "light" messages emitted by matter, much like we study materials in a laboratory. Chemistry is a powerful tool not only for understanding phenomena on Earth but also for unraveling the mysteries of the universe.
