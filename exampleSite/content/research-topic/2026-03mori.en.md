### Skyrmions: Magnetic Swirls in the Microscopic World
Skyrmions are special magnetic structures, like tiny “magnetic particles,” where the direction of magnetism is arranged in a swirling pattern. These skyrmions are very small and stable, and they can be moved by electrical currents, making them promising candidates for next-generation memory and information processing devices. In this research, we theoretically investigated how information is exchanged within a system containing two of these skyrmions (a two-skyrmion system).

### Decoding Information Flow with “Transfer Entropy”
Information flows all around us. For example, when one event influences another, there is a flow of information. To mathematically analyze this flow, we used tools from information theory such as “Shannon entropy” and “mutual information.” We particularly focused on “transfer entropy,” an important measure that quantitatively indicates how much information has been transferred from one system to another. This study elucidates the physical significance of transfer entropy, which has often been overlooked in previous research.

### Asymmetric Information Exchange in Skyrmion Systems
Numerical simulations (simulations: reproducing real-world phenomena on a computer) revealed that the motion of skyrmions is not merely random but exhibits highly complex and “nontrivial dynamics.” Specifically, due to the unique property of “chiral motion” (chiral motion: asymmetric movement, like a left- or right-handed screw) inherent in skyrmions, information flows asymmetrically between the two skyrmions. This means that the ease of information transfer in one direction differs from that in the reverse direction. This finding implies a violation of the “detailed balance condition” (detailed balance condition: the condition that the probability of a microscopic process and its reverse process are equal), highlighting the unique properties of skyrmion systems.

### Towards Future “Natural Computing”
Our analysis of transfer entropy also showed that the characteristic time required for information transfer (the time it takes for a skyrmion state to change) depends on the size of the box confining the skyrmions, not on the interaction range between them. A two-skyrmion system with such “unusual asymmetric circulation of information” holds the potential for applications in a new type of device called “natural computing,” which autonomously processes information much like the human brain. This research elucidates the unique information processing capabilities of skyrmions, contributing to the development of future computing technologies.
