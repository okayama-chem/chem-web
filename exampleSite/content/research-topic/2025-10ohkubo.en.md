### The Mystery of Cement and "Carbonation"
Cement is a crucial material supporting our infrastructure, from buildings to bridges. However, it can react with carbon dioxide (CO₂) in the air, leading to a phenomenon called "carbonation," which can degrade the cement. While carbonation reduces cement's durability, it also involves the absorption of CO₂. In this research, we investigated the detailed mechanism of how cement undergoes carbonation.

### Water Movement: Key to Carbonation Progression
To understand how carbonation progresses, we closely observed the "water content distribution" and "changes in crystal structure" within cement pastes (a mixture of cement and water) using specialized techniques: proton nuclear magnetic resonance relaxometry (for tracking water) and micro-X-ray diffraction (for observing mineral phases). We discovered that water movement in cement significantly influences carbonation progression. Specifically, we found that the "drying front" (where water content decreases) closely aligned with the "carbonation front" (where carbonation occurs). This suggests that the spaces created by water evaporation act as pathways for CO₂ ingress, pushing carbonation forward like a "plugging effect."

### Discovering the "Limit" of Carbonation
Further investigation revealed that carbonation progression stops once the "empty space" (porosity) within the cement increases to approximately 18%. This occurs because calcium (alumino) silicate hydrate (C-(A)-S-H), a primary component of cement, reacts with CO₂ and decomposes. The remaining products then agglomerate (clump together), preventing further CO₂ penetration. This "18% empty space" limit was consistently observed across various cement pastes, even those with different calcium to silicon/aluminum ratios.

### Towards Future Sustainable Cement Materials
The findings from this study are crucial for deeply understanding the mechanism of cement carbonation. Our simplified model allows for accurate prediction of carbonation depth, which is valuable for developing materials with enhanced durability and for assessing the CO₂ uptake capacity of cement. This research is expected to contribute to the development of new, sustainable cementitious materials that support a resilient society.
