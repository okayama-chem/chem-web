### From Inhibition to Degradation: A New Era in Drug Discovery

Many existing drugs work by "inhibiting," or blocking the function of, specific proteins that cause diseases. However, what if we could not just block their function, but actually "destroy and remove" these proteins from cells? This could lead to even more effective treatments.

This research review summarizes a significant shift in modern drug discovery: moving from the traditional approach of "inhibiting" protein function to a new strategy of "targeted degradation." The key to this innovative approach lies in a new class of molecules called "PROTACs."

### What are PROTACs?

PROTACs (Proteolysis-Targeting Chimeras) are special molecules that act as a "bridge." They connect a specific target protein that we want to eliminate with the cell's natural waste disposal system, called the "proteasome." By bringing these two together, PROTACs cause the target protein to be broken down and efficiently removed from the cell.

A remarkable advantage of this method is its potential to modulate both the "catalytic functions" (the ability to accelerate chemical reactions) and "non-catalytic functions" (other roles not directly related to chemical reactions) of proteins. This is often difficult to achieve with traditional inhibitors.

### Targeting Epigenetic Enzymes for Disease Treatment

Epigenetic enzymes are crucial proteins that regulate how our genes work, and they are known to be involved in many diseases, including cancer. This makes them very important "targets" for developing new therapeutic drugs.

This review highlights specific case studies focusing on PROTACs designed for epigenetic enzymes such as HDAC8, G9a/GLP, and KDM5. These examples demonstrate the successful transition from simply inhibiting these enzymes to selectively degrading them, opening up new possibilities for treatment that were previously unattainable with conventional inhibitors.

### Challenges and Future Prospects

PROTACs offer a groundbreaking mechanism of action that is different from traditional drugs, holding immense promise for the development of more effective and specific therapies with fewer side effects. While there are still challenges to overcome, this technology is highly anticipated in modern pharmaceutical research and has the potential to significantly transform future medicine.
