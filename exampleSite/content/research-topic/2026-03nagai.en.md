### Understanding Molecular Motion: Beyond Global Averages

When you drop ink into water, it eventually spreads throughout. This phenomenon is called "diffusion," a fundamental process where molecules move from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration. All matter around us is made of tiny molecules constantly in motion, and this molecular movement is crucial for understanding material properties and chemical reactions.

Typically, the "diffusion coefficient" — a measure of how fast molecules move — describes the average motion across the entire substance (global diffusion). However, in "heterogeneous systems," like the boundary between oil and water or inside a living cell, the environment varies significantly from place to place. In such systems, molecules are expected to move differently depending on their exact location. Understanding these "local movements" is extremely important for fields ranging from biological processes to the development of new functional materials.

### Unraveling Local Motion: A Novel Analytical Method

In computational simulations of molecular motion, the "Mean Square Displacement (MSD)" is a commonly used metric. It quantifies how far molecules travel on average over a given time. For global diffusion, the regular MSD increases linearly with time, and its slope directly gives the global diffusion coefficient.

However, when we focus on a specific, confined region (subspace) of the system, the "local Mean Square Displacement" exhibits a complex "nonlinear relationship" with time. This nonlinearity makes it challenging to accurately extract the "local diffusion coefficient" from this data.

To overcome this, we developed a new "analytical expression" describing the nonlinear behavior of local MSD, employing a sophisticated mathematical technique called "Edgeworth expansion" up to the fourth order. By combining this expression with "regression analysis," we can now analyze complex local molecular motions more accurately and efficiently than before.

### From Simple to Complex: Demonstrating the New Analysis

To validate the effectiveness of our new analytical approach, we first applied it to a relatively simple system: a "hydrogen molecule" diffusing in "homogeneous bulk water." The results showed that we could estimate the local diffusion coefficients with a deviation of less than 20% from the standard reference values, even at a high spatial resolution of 0.23 nanometers (one nanometer is a billionth of a meter).

Next, we tackled a more complex "heterogeneous system": the diffusion of a "methane molecule" in an "aqueous solution near a hydrophobic interface" (a boundary that repels water). Here too, our method yielded local diffusion coefficients that were in good agreement with previous studies. However, in a specific region approximately 0.6 nanometers from the interface, the influence of higher-order terms of the Edgeworth expansion (more complex factors), which were omitted in this work, became significant. While resolving this limitation is a challenge for future research, our study clearly demonstrates the broad utility of this method in understanding molecular motion across various environments.

### The Future of Material Science Through Theoretical and Computational Chemistry

This research highlights the exciting potential of "theoretical and computational chemistry," where we combine computer simulations with advanced mathematics to gain deep insights into molecular-level phenomena. Molecular motion is at the heart of many processes, such as how drugs are delivered in the body or how ions move within battery materials.

Our novel analytical method promises to uncover previously overlooked "local movements" of molecules, potentially leading to new discoveries in material science and life sciences.
