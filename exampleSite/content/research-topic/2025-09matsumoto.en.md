### Challenges in CO2 Transport: The Unexpected Role of Water and Hydrates

As a measure against global warming, technologies for capturing carbon dioxide (CO2) emitted from factories and storing it deep underground in liquid form, or transporting it over long distances, are gaining attention. However, there's a tricky problem lurking beneath the surface when it comes to efficiently and safely transporting this CO2: the small amount of "water" dissolved in liquid CO2 and the "hydrate" that it can form.

A hydrate is a crystalline substance where water molecules form cage-like structures, trapping gas molecules (in this case, CO2) inside. If too much water dissolves in liquid CO2, these hydrates can form under the cold or high-pressure conditions of transport, leading to pipeline blockages or corrosion of transport vessels.

### Unveiling Water's Behavior with Theoretical Chemistry

Accurately predicting how liquid CO2, water, and hydrate interact, and how much water dissolves in CO2, has been a difficult challenge. This is especially true under extreme conditions of low temperature and high pressure where hydrates form, making experimental investigation very challenging.

Our research group tackled this mystery using "theoretical calculations" on computers. By calculating the "chemical potentials" (an indicator of a substance's stability) for water, hydrate, and liquid CO2, we developed a new method to predict how much water dissolves in CO2 (solubility) under various temperature and pressure conditions. A major advantage of this method is its ability to accurately investigate water behavior even in harsh environments where hydrates are formed.

### How Hydrates Drastically Alter Water Solubility

Our calculations first showed a remarkable success: our theoretical model could accurately reproduce the trends of water solubility observed in previous experiments. This indicates that our computational model precisely captures real-world phenomena.

Even more significant was another finding: the amount of water dissolved in liquid CO2 drastically decreases when hydrate coexists, compared to when it's absent. This happens because the hydrate strongly attracts and traps water molecules, leaving less water available to dissolve into the liquid CO2.

Especially at low temperatures, liquid CO2 loses its capacity to retain water more seriously than anticipated, leading to excessive water precipitation into hydrate. This means that hydrate is not just a "cause of blockage," but a "key player" that fundamentally changes the behavior of water in liquid CO2.

### Ensuring Safety in CO2 Transport

The accurate information on water solubility obtained from this research is extremely valuable for enhancing the safety of large-scale CO2 transport. For example, knowing at what temperatures and pressures hydrates are likely to form, and how the amount of water in liquid CO2 changes under those conditions, allows for effective avoidance of blockage and corrosion risks in pipeline design and operation.

Our theoretical chemistry research has the potential to unravel the unseen world of molecules and contribute to solving major societal challenges like global warming.
