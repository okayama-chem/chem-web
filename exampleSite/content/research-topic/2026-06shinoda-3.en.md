### Amphotericin B: A Potent Drug Against Fungal Infections

Amphotericin B (AmB) is a crucial drug used to treat systemic fungal infections. It is believed to work by preferentially binding to a lipid called "ergosterol" in fungal cell membranes, forming ion-permeable pores (holes) that kill the fungus. Human cell membranes contain a similar lipid called "cholesterol," but AmB binds more strongly to ergosterol, allowing it to selectively target fungi and reduce side effects. However, the detailed mechanism of how AmB binds to ergosterol and forms stable channels has remained largely unknown.

### The Enigma of the Key Sugar: Mycosamine

The AmB molecule has a unique amino sugar, "mycosamine," attached to it. This mycosamine has been proposed to mediate specific interactions with ergosterol. However, determining the precise "orientation" of mycosamine within cell membranes has been a significant challenge, especially because the conformations (shapes) of glycosidic linkages (the bonds connecting sugars) are difficult to analyze in complex lipid bilayers.

### Isotopic Labeling and Simulations Reveal Sugar Orientation

Our research group developed a novel "isotopic editing strategy." This technique involves introducing atoms of different masses (isotopes, such as deuterium or carbon-13) at specific positions within the AmB molecule. By using this labeled AmB, we performed measurements using "solid-state NMR," a powerful technique for analyzing the structure of solid materials.
The results showed that in the presence of ergosterol, a specific signal from the mycosamine sugar became significantly sharper, indicating that the sugar's orientation was strongly restricted. Furthermore, "all-atom molecular dynamics simulations" (computer simulations that track the movement of individual atoms) revealed that when AmB binds to ergosterol, it forms stronger and more stable associations compared to binding with cholesterol. The simulations also showed narrower distributions for the "dihedral angles" (angles describing the rotation around chemical bonds) of the mycosamine moiety, indicating less flexibility.

### Channel Clustering Enhances Stability

The simulations also revealed that when AmB molecules assemble to form channels, their "antiparallel" association (a specific head-to-tail arrangement) leads to enhanced stability of the channel assemblies in the presence of ergosterol. This phenomenon is driven by strong "hydrogen bonds" (weak attractive forces between molecules) between AmB and ergosterol. Moreover, a "synergistic effect" was suggested, where the clustering of AmB channels further stabilizes their active ion channel architecture.
These findings provide the most direct structural insights to date into how AmB selectively acts on fungi and forms pores in their cell membranes. This knowledge could pave the way for designing safer and more effective new antifungal drugs.
